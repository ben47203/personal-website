My Travels Through Europe So Far█

June 2025 8 min read

I can't believe it's already been 10 days since I departed on my roughly seven-week trip throughout Europe. It started with a 13-hour flight to Dubai on one of Emirates' many A380s. It is so big that as you are going down the runway, you second-guess if this thing can even fly until it takes to the air at the last second. Then, they serve you dinner with real silverware — Qantas and Jetstar seriously need to pick up their game. I decided to opt for a 10-hour layover in Dubai so I would have time to explore the city as it worked well with daylight hours.

Dubai

Two main things surprised me about the city. First — and this sounds silly to say — but unlike what social media would lead you to believe, not everyone is driving supercars. There is a significant working-class population, especially construction workers, keeping the city moving. Second, the haze. Maybe it was just the weather, but on the day visibility was significantly reduced and you could not see the marina skyscrapers from the CBD due to the dust.

The big palm-tree-shaped island was very impressive, but still heavily under construction. I saw Bugattis, AMG Ones, and SVJs in showroom windows on the metro back to the Burj Khalifa, which of course I also went up. And they weren't wrong — it is a very tall building. You look up and expect it to stop, but it just keeps on reaching higher into the sky. I met a bloke from Perth at the top and we enjoyed the views before I headed back to the airport. I understand the appeal of the city — low taxes, cheap, luxurious property, decent cost of living — but feel it lacks a certain depth of culture and authenticity.

Athens

I got in late, but had company from TJ and some girls from Sacramento on the metro into the city. The very first thing you notice is the cats — there are cats everywhere in Greece; they just walk the streets and people even leave food out for them. The next day I went for a wander and stumbled upon the Panathenaic Stadium and got a glimpse of the Acropolis. The city viewed from a vantage point is just as you imagine, but down on street level there is unfortunately a decent amount of litter and graffiti, though it remains vibrant with people around every corner.

On the beautiful five-hour bus ride to Lefkada I sat next to a Greek guy named Dimitrish, same age as me, who had just come back from compulsory military service earning 80 euros a month. It struck me how fortunate I am.

On the Abby Rose

In Lefkada I met up with Anna and Owen, my great-aunt and uncle, to stay for four nights on their boat, the Abby Rose. It is a very cool boat — a monohull, built like a tank with an aluminium hull, and a retractable keel to go in shallow waters. Some highlights from the five days:

  • Lots of traditional Greek food — gyros, meat platters, squid, moussaka (basically Greek eggplant lasagne), and a whole grilled fish on the first night at a family restaurant on a beach with only three tables.
  • Lots of swimming at beautiful coves and bays.
  • Hiking up to see Homer's school and Odysseus' palace.
  • Getting the asymmetric spinnaker up and doing some sailing.
  • Pulling off an awesome manoeuvre of simultaneously pulling the anchor in and releasing a shore line to avoid getting blown onto a very nice motor yacht.
  • Going on a boat tour of the Melissani caves.
  • Seeing the amazingly intricate and ornate churches at remote communities.

I caught a propeller plane back to Athens which was very cool, but I wouldn't rush to do it again thanks to the noise and vibration.

Back in Athens

I stayed one more night in Athens so I could see the Acropolis. It was cool, pretty much exactly what you would expect. Interestingly, they are rebuilding it by categorising all the fallen pieces of stone and reassembling it with fabricated concrete to replace what was lost. Since otherwise it would have collapsed, I think this is a good approach to retain the honour of it. This also reflects an interesting nationwide approach to history — they simply have so much of it they can't preserve it all. You could literally walk in and throughout Homer's school.
