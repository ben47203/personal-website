My Memorable Time at Lady Margaret Hall, Oxford█

July 2025 10 min read

I have just finished up my three-week programme at the beautiful Lady Margaret Hall college, part of the University of Oxford. It was an amazing experience that I am so glad to have had the opportunity to participate in.

People from over 50 countries took part, so I had the privilege of making new friends all around the world from countries including the United States, the United Kingdom, France, the Netherlands, Spain, Switzerland, Denmark, Sweden, Ukraine, Russia, the Czech Republic, South Africa, India, Pakistan, China, Taiwan, South Korea, Vietnam, New Zealand, and Australia.

Activities

From the beginning it was very easy to meet people, at meal times or just around the college, but the programme also did a great job of running social events. The first Saturday was an organised day trip to Stratford-upon-Avon, famously the town where Shakespeare was born. Unfortunately, 'the birthplace' was very underwhelming — it was just a particularly well-preserved mediaeval house with some done-up rooms to mimic what it might have looked like. Funnily enough, Shakespeare didn't even write any plays in the house, because he left at 18. Thankfully, the town has a little picturesque British river running through it, and they let you rent 3-knot electric boats without so much as a safety briefing.

Continuing on the maritime theme, we were able to go punting on the river next to Lady Margaret Hall. Punting involves pushing a long, flat, wooden boat along a shallow river with a long stick. The resistance against the riverbed acts as propulsion instead of rowing; it is a popular activity and attraction in both Oxford and Cambridge. It was challenging at first, but quite fun once I got the hang of it. On my second go, I got a bit overconfident, jammed the stick in the riverbed too hard, it got stuck, and I tried to pull it back but ended up pulling myself in.

The programme provided us with a tour of the Ashmolean Museum with one of the lead curators, which was very interesting. I also participated in a mediaeval manuscripts viewing session — it is amazing how well they were preserved. Additionally, we had guest lectures on literature and science and a drinks reception in the Pitt Rivers Museum.

A highlight was being able to participate in a debate in the Oxford Union chambers. People who have spoken in the same chambers include Queen Elizabeth the Second, Winston Churchill, Ronald Reagan, the Dalai Lama, Albert Einstein, and Elton John. I argued against the motion that "This house believes artificial intelligence undermines human creativity and innovation" — I feel I presented a strong closing argument, but unfortunately we were not able to convince a majority.

We also had a day trip to the British Museum in London. They had so much stuff, it was almost overwhelming. The items I found the most interesting were traditional textiles from Afghanistan — since the country has been in conflict for so long and remains very underdeveloped, imagery of modern military equipment is present in traditionally woven textiles. While in London I did my tourist duties of visiting the bridge, the palace, Westminster Abbey, Big Ben, and, inadvertently, the Beatles' crosswalk.

Coursework

The course I studied was "Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning: Theory and Practice," and I was very impressed with the quality of teaching. We would have daily lectures and seminars from a professor visiting from KAUST and a tutor who had completed postdoctoral research at Oxford. On Fridays we would have small-group tutorials to go over assessments.

It started with multi-parameter linear regressions — for example, predicting Walmart sales based on store, day, unemployment, inflation, and so on. This would be done by making a loss function to calculate the error and how much each weighting contributes to it, then deriving it, setting it to zero, and solving the large system of linear equations using matrix multiplication to find the local minimum of loss.

Using this principle, we created neural networks by having multiple linear functions applied to the inputs over multiple layers, activated by nonlinear functions between them so that the composition is meaningful, and initialised with random weights. Since you cannot simply compute the local minimum of a highly dimensional space, the gradient of the loss function for each neuron indicates which direction you need to adjust its weightings to minimise loss — this is called gradient descent. This is how artificial intelligence "learns," and by setting inputs to pixels in an image and outputs to probabilities, you can perform tasks such as image recognition.

Basically, there is no magic going on with AI; it is all just maths, and it is amazing how quickly and easily you can implement it.

Oxford

Oxford was a great place to study. Even outside of term, it had a vibrant energy thanks to the youthful demographic and surprising amount of tourists. Lady Margaret Hall was about a 20-minute walk, via the beautiful university parks, to the city centre. It truly lives up to its reputation — there are buildings that are hundreds and hundreds of years old around every corner. Underneath one bridge and down a very narrow twisting alleyway was a 600-plus-year-old pub.

Following the theme of the previous posts, the people in the programme truly made it special — not so much the place. At the end of the day it is just a university. Oxford may be more successful and have marginally better quality of teaching than others, but I am sure that even during the term, it is the unique and interesting students that bring it to life.
