*Paris*
On the day my programme in Oxford finished, I took the Eurostar to Paris. Unfortunately I was on the newer e320 model by Siemens, which has a higher top speed but is far less stylish than the older, refurbished e300s – you can’t win them all. 

The next morning I met up with some friends I met in Oxford to go sightseeing in Paris. The day started with an invitation to attend Mass in the Notre Dame, which I naively neglected would be in French until I was walking in; I learnt a French word: ‘résurrection’ – pretty self-explanatory, but that is about all I understood. The building is beautiful after the restoration, and its style, combined with being in France, is definitely better suited to the original light sandstone. 

Next was the Eiffel Tower, which we climbed halfway up using the stairs, which was surprisingly good value. There were great views of Paris all the way up, and it was certainly far taller than I expected. When I returned to my hostel, every other person in the 8-bed rooms was there and awake – which is almost unheard of. Everyone got along well; we met others in the hostel bar, and with a group of about 20, we headed to the Eiffel Tower to enjoy the night. A great end to my time in Paris.

*Mt Ventoux*
Early the next day I caught a TGV (French high-speed train) down to Avignon and began to prepare for an adventure. To complement the cheapest sleeping bag, tarp, and mat I could find in Paris, I provisioned 7 litres of water, some tinned salmon, apples, carrots, and bread. From there I caught a regional train for an hour, and then a random minibus that comes once every three hours for 1 euro, and then hiked a few kilometres to the base of Mt Ventoux.    When planning my trip, I realised that my time in France would coincide with the Tour de France, and a stage with an iconic mountaintop finish that I remember watching before with my dad caught my eye. So what better way to go on an adventure and see the iconic race than climbing this mountain? However, because of the tour, all the accomodation was booked out anywhere near the mountain. So I took matters into my own hands. That day I climbed a few hundred metres up, found a small clearing beside the trail, and set up my tarp with some string.   The next morning I woke up freezing and hiked the remaining distance of the 1900m elevation mountain with my ~28kg pack and saw the Tour. There were heaps of people there, an impressive entourage before the riders, and then finally the race crossed the summit at a crazy speed. That night I managed to find the same place again before dark and settled in. Soon after, I heard oinking and rustling; this area of France is infested with wild pigs. But I stayed dead quiet and prayed, and thankfully they went the other way. This happened once again on the other side of me, but thankfully I never ended up seeing any wild pigs and made it to the bus stop the next day, just in time to do the same journey in reverse with a TGV along the coast to Nice.

*Nice*
Nice was nice.

*Monaco*
After staying a night in Nice, I passed through Monaco for a day. It was a very interesting city that fits its stereotype well. It is spotless, with countless glistening apartment buildings overlooking the iconic Marina. There wasn’t much I could afford to do apart from taking in the views of the city, seeing the Monte Carlo Casino, and supercar spotting. Here the density of supercars was crazy, much higher than Dubai or anywhere else I have seen, but still not as many as social media would have you think.
￼

*Uscio*
Leaving Monaco, I thought I would be heading to Portofino, a quaint Italian seaside town. However, when I checked the location of my accommodation, I realised I had booked simply in the Portofino municipality and instead took a bus for an hour and a half, up 380m of elevation, into a tiny, steep, hillside town called Uscio. Here I was staying in a very authentic hostel, ran out of a house, with only about 10 beds. There was only one restaurant in the town, so I got a pizza and shared dinner with all the others staying here. It was a really nice experience and a great change of pace from the capital cities.

The next day I did end up going to the town of Portofino with Faith and Brandon, two friends I met at the hostel. It was a truly beautiful part of the world that was, unfortunately, but very understandably, quite touristy. Regardless, we made the most of the attractions, gazing over the amazing views from the old castle and then getting some pasta and taking the ferry back to another town where we did a bit more exploring. After returning later that night, English pop music started blaring in this tiny town. As it turns out, there was a DJ in the town square for a whole town dance party, which was very funny. So all the hostel guests ended up doing the YMCA with the locals.

*Cinque Terra*
The next stop was the Cinque Terra, which translates to the 5 towns, and consists of 5 very picturesque towns along the Italian coast. They all have dense, multicoloured houses on steep rocks and are the first image that comes up when you search “Italian seaside town”. I took this stage of my trip at a slower pace, relaxing, swimming, cliff jumping and hiking. On the ~15km hike between the towns I ran into one of the people staying in my room, and he had his drone with him, so we got some amazing pictures from the top of one of the cliffs. It was certainly one of the most beautiful hikes I have done. While staying here I met some people from New York and Melbourne who contributed to making this section a very enjoyable part of my journey.

*Pisa*
I stopped in for a bit over an hour on my way to Rome, and that was honestly probably too long. Pisa is a small, underwhelming town, and really the only main attraction is the tower, with the side dishes of the cathedral and baptistery to complement it. It was quite funny; the streets were all very quiet right up until I turned the corner into the main square with the tower, and then there were tourists everywhere, all trying to get that perfect photo supporting the tower. To be fair, the tower was pretty cool. It is on a much steeper slant than what it looks like in photos or you expect.
￼

*Florence*
Also fit the lively city of Florence into the same day. I started with the cathedral, which was definitely up there with the Cologne Cathedral and Notre Dame for the best I had seen yet with its green and pink marble, but still nothing compared to what was to come in Rome. The entire city was like the old town of others I had visited, with winding cobbled streets and dense buildings, which made exploring interesting. There was a replica of David by Michelangelo in a fountain, the former place of the original, and many art installations throughout. 

Along with every other tourist, I made the pilgrimage to the Piazzale Michelangelo, where there is the stunning, iconic, panoramic view of the city. From here I meandered back to the train station via the golden bridge and Leather Street, named for its many, questionably authentic, street vendors.
￼
￼

*Rome*
Rome was the final location of my 7-week journey, and my time mostly consisted of exploring the ancient attractions it is known for. Coincidentally my visit coincided with the Catholic Jubilee, meaning that many young Catholics from around the world were coming to make a pilgrimage to the churches and attractions. The crowds were insane, probably the biggest I had ever been in.  I visited plenty of churches, and they were beyond words. St Peter’s Basilica was incredibly large with impossibly intricate mosaics and a huge crowd to match. The Vatican Museum exceeded expectations with more wonderful art than I had probably seen previously in my entire life combined. The Colosseum, Trevi Fountain, Palatine Hill, and the various other attractions were all thoroughly interesting.
