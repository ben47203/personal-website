**My memorable time at Lady Margaret Hall, Oxford.**

Hello everyone!  I have just finished up my 3-week programme at the beautiful Lady Margaret Hall college, part of the University of Oxford. It was an amazing experience that I am so glad to have had the opportunity to participate in. 

People from over 50 countries took part, so I had the privilege of making new friends all around the world from countries including the United States, the United Kingdom, France, the Netherlands, mi amigos from Spain, Switzerland, Denmark, Sweden, Ukraine, Russia, the Czech Republic, South Africa, India, Pakistan, China, Taiwan, South Korea, Vietnam, New Zealand, and Australia (photo of all the Australians below). Shoutout to all the people now on this list who I met at the programme, I greatly enjoyed my time with you all and hope to reconnect with you all one day—let me know if you are ever in Australia!



*Activities

From the beginning it was very easy to meet people, at meal times or just around the college, but the programme also did a great job of running social events. The first Saturday was an organised day trip to Stratford-upon-Avon, famously the town where Shakespeare was born. Unfortunately, “the birthplace,” as they call it (of Shakespeare), was very underwhelming. It was just a particularly well-preserved mediaeval house with some done-up rooms to mimic what it might have looked like. Funnily enough, Shakespeare didn’t even write any plays in the house, because he left the house at 18. Thankfully, the town has a little, picturesque British river running through it, and they let you rent 3-knot electric boats without so much as a safety briefing; we saw long boats, ducks, and swans.

Continuing on the maritime theme, we were able to go punting on the river next to Lady Margaret Hall. Punting involves pushing a long, flat, wooden boat along a shallow river with a long stick. The resistance against the riverbed acts as propulsion instead of rowing; it is a popular activity and attraction in both Oxford and Cambridge—maybe UQ needs some punts to up our ranking! It was challenging at first, but quite fun once I got the hang of it. On my second go with some girls from America and Australia, I got a bit overconfident, jammed the stick in the riverbed too hard, it got stuck, and I tried to pull it back but ended up pulling myself in. See photos of me on the punt and in the river below.




Moving on to more intellectual endeavours, because it is Oxford after all, the program provided us with a tour of the Ashmolean Museum with one of the lead curators, which was very interesting. I also participated in a mediaeval manuscripts viewing session. It is amazing how well they were preserved; the one photographed below is a commentary on the book of Ecclesiastes, which belonged to the library of a monastery, secured by a chain in the hole in the bottom. Additionally, we had guest lectures on literature and science and a drinks reception in the Pitt Rivers Museum.

A highlight was being able to participate in a debate in the Oxford Union chambers organised by the college. People who have spoken in the same chambers include Queen Elizabeth the Second, Winston Churchill, Ronald Reagan, the Dalai Lama, Albert Einstein, and Elton John. I argued against the motion that “This house believes artificial intelligence undermines human creativity and innovation”; I feel I presented a strong closing argument, but unfortunately, we were not able to convince a majority. The second debate was related to museums returning artefacts. I was tending against museums returning artefacts; however, the next day we had a day trip to the British Museum.

The British Museum had so much stuff, it was almost overwhelming. They definitely could have returned some of that stuff to Egypt, and no one would have missed it. The items I found the most interesting were traditional textiles from Afghanistan. Since the country has been in conflict for so long, and it is still very underdeveloped, imagery of modern military equipment is present in traditionally woven textiles. While in London I did my tourist duties of visiting the bridge, the palace, Westminster Abbey, Big Ben, and, inadvertently, the Beatles’ crosswalk.




*Coursework*
The course I studied was “Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning: Theory and Practice,” and I was very impressed with the quality of teaching. We would have daily lectures and seminars from a professor visiting from KAUST and a tutor who had completed postdoctoral research at Oxford, respectively. On Fridays we would have small group tutorials to go over assessments (pictured below).

It started with multi-parameter linear regressions, for example, predicting Walmart sales based on store, day, unemployment, inflation, etc. This would be done by making a loss function to calculate the error and how much each weighting contributes to it, then deriving it, setting it to zero, and solving the large system of linear equations using matrix multiplication to find the local minimum of loss. Using this principle, we created neural networks by having multiple linear functions applied to the inputs over multiple layers, activated by nonlinear functions between (so the composition is meaningful), and initialised with random weights. Since you cannot simply compute the local minimum of a highly dimensional space, the gradient of the loss function for each neuron (linear function) indicates which direction you need to adjust its weightings to minimise loss; this is called gradient descent. This is how artificial intelligence “learns,” and by setting inputs to say pixels in an image and outputs to probabilities, you can perform tasks such as image recognition.

I hope that made sense. Basically, there is no magic going on with AI; it is all just math, and it’s amazing how quickly and easily you can implement it.


*Oxford*
Oxford was a great place to study. Even outside of term, it had a vibrant energy, thanks to the youthful demographic and surprising amount of tourists. Lady Margaret Hall was about a 20-minute walk, via the beautiful university parks, to the city centre, which was quite nice because it was a very walkable city with lots of scenic buildings. In this regard it truly lives up to its reputation; there are buildings that are hundreds and hundreds of years old around every corner. Underneath the bridge (pictured below) and down a very narrow twisting alleyway was a 600+ year old pub. It surprised me that there was a strong drinking culture, with lots of pubs. It’s a wonder how any study gets done. Even the college has a full-service bar on campus! 

I was also fortunate enough to find a very nice church to attend during my time there. It was full of wonderful people of all ages from all around the world. Similarly, and following the theme of the previous emails, the people in the programme truly made it special—not so much the place. At the end of the day it is just a university. Oxford may be more successful and have marginally better quality of teaching than others, but I’m sure that even during the term, it is the unique and interesting students (of whom I had the pleasure of meeting a few) that bring it to life. Thank you to everyone in the program who made it as enjoyable and special as it was.
