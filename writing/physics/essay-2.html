Thermodynamics of Black Holes█

March 2026 Physics

Black holes, once considered mere mathematical curiosities of general relativity, have emerged as central objects in modern theoretical physics. The discovery that black holes possess thermodynamic properties — temperature, entropy, and the capacity to radiate — has profound implications for our understanding of quantum gravity and the nature of information. This essay surveys the key developments in black hole thermodynamics and their significance.

The foundations were laid by Jacob Bekenstein in 1972, who proposed that black holes carry entropy proportional to the area of their event horizon. This was a radical suggestion: in classical general relativity, a black hole is characterised by only three parameters — mass, charge, and angular momentum — leaving no obvious microscopic degrees of freedom to account for entropy. Bekenstein's insight drew on the observation that the area of an event horizon never decreases in classical processes, mirroring the second law of thermodynamics.

Stephen Hawking's 1974 calculation transformed this analogy into a precise physical statement. By applying quantum field theory in curved spacetime, Hawking demonstrated that black holes emit thermal radiation at a temperature inversely proportional to their mass. This Hawking radiation implies that black holes are not truly black but slowly evaporate, losing mass and eventually disappearing entirely. The thermal spectrum of this radiation raised the information paradox: if a black hole forms from a pure quantum state and evaporates into featureless thermal radiation, unitarity — a cornerstone of quantum mechanics — appears to be violated.

The information paradox has driven much of the theoretical physics research of the past five decades. Proposed resolutions range from remnants that store information indefinitely, to modifications of quantum mechanics, to the holographic principle articulated by 't Hooft and Susskind, which suggests that all information within a volume of space can be encoded on its boundary. Recent developments involving the Page curve, quantum extremal surfaces, and the island formula have provided compelling evidence that information is indeed preserved, though the precise mechanism remains an active area of investigation at the intersection of quantum mechanics and general relativity.
