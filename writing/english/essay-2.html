Rhetoric and Persuasion in Political Speech█

May 2026 English

Political speeches occupy a unique position in public discourse, combining literary craft with immediate persuasive intent. This essay analyses the rhetorical strategies employed in landmark Australian political speeches, examining how speakers deploy classical techniques of ethos, pathos, and logos to shape public opinion and construct political identity.

Paul Keating's 1992 Redfern Address represents a masterclass in rhetorical reframing. By repeatedly using the pronoun "we" to implicate non-Indigenous Australians in historical violence — "We took the traditional lands," "We brought the diseases" — Keating transforms abstract historical injustice into personal collective responsibility. The anaphoric structure creates a litany that accumulates emotional weight while simultaneously performing the acknowledgment it advocates.

Julia Gillard's 2012 misogyny speech demonstrates how kairos — the exploitation of rhetorical timing — can transform parliamentary procedure into cultural event. By redirecting a motion about sexism back toward the opposition leader's own conduct, Gillard employs the classical technique of retorsio, turning an opponent's argument against them. The speech's viral circulation beyond parliament illustrates how digital media has altered the relationship between rhetorical occasion and audience.

These examples reveal that effective political rhetoric in the Australian context often operates through disruption of established discursive norms rather than adherence to them. Both speeches derived their power from breaking with expected parliamentary register — Keating through unprecedented honesty about colonial violence, Gillard through direct confrontation where diplomatic circumlocution was expected.
