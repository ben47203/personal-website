Postcolonial Narratives in Australian Literature█

March 2026 English

Australian literature has long grappled with the legacy of colonisation, producing a rich body of work that interrogates settler identity, Indigenous dispossession, and the construction of national mythology. This essay examines how contemporary Australian authors engage with postcolonial theory to challenge dominant historical narratives and centre Indigenous perspectives.

The pastoral tradition in Australian literature — from Henry Lawson to Patrick White — constructed the outback as empty space awaiting European cultivation. Postcolonial readings reveal this as a literary enactment of terra nullius, erasing tens of thousands of years of Indigenous habitation and land management. Contemporary writers such as Alexis Wright and Kim Scott have responded by foregrounding Indigenous cosmologies and oral traditions within the novel form, creating works that resist assimilation into Western literary frameworks.

Wright's Carpentaria exemplifies this approach, weaving Waanyi storytelling traditions into a narrative that refuses linear chronology and individual heroism. The novel's structure mirrors the cyclical temporality of Indigenous knowledge systems, challenging readers accustomed to European narrative conventions. Similarly, Scott's That Deadman Dance recovers Noongar history through a protagonist who moves between cultures, exposing the violence that colonial "progress" narratives obscure.

These works suggest that postcolonial Australian literature is not merely about representing Indigenous experience for non-Indigenous audiences, but about transforming the literary forms inherited from colonialism itself. The challenge for criticism is to develop frameworks adequate to these innovations without domesticating them within existing theoretical categories.
