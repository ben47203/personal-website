Supply and Demand in Australian Housing█

March 2026

The Australian housing market has long been a subject of intense scrutiny, both domestically and internationally. Over the past two decades, prices in major metropolitan areas — particularly Sydney and Melbourne — have risen at rates far exceeding wage growth, creating significant affordability challenges for first-home buyers and renters alike. This essay examines the structural supply and demand factors underpinning these trends.

On the demand side, population growth driven by immigration has been a primary catalyst. Australia's net overseas migration has consistently added hundreds of thousands of new residents each year, concentrating overwhelmingly in capital cities where employment opportunities and services are most accessible. Coupled with historically low interest rates maintained by the Reserve Bank of Australia through much of the 2010s, borrowing capacity expanded dramatically, pushing up the prices that buyers were willing and able to pay.

Supply-side constraints have compounded these demand pressures. Restrictive zoning regulations, lengthy development approval processes, and infrastructure bottlenecks have limited the pace of new housing construction. Land release on the urban fringe, while politically popular, often occurs without corresponding investment in transport and social infrastructure, creating dormitory suburbs with limited amenity. Meanwhile, infill development in established suburbs faces significant community opposition.

Policy responses have varied in their effectiveness. Demand-side measures such as the First Home Owner Grant and stamp duty concessions have been criticised for simply inflating prices further, while supply-side reforms have proven politically difficult to implement at the local government level where planning decisions are made. A more integrated approach, combining planning reform with targeted infrastructure investment and carefully calibrated fiscal policy, is likely necessary to address the affordability crisis in a sustained manner.
