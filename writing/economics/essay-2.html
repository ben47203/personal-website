Monetary Policy and Inflation Targeting█

April 2026 Economics

Since the early 1990s, the Reserve Bank of Australia has pursued an inflation-targeting framework, aiming to keep consumer price inflation between two and three per cent on average over the economic cycle. This approach, adopted in the wake of the early-1990s recession, represented a significant shift in Australian monetary policy and has been broadly credited with anchoring inflation expectations and contributing to the long period of economic stability that followed.

The mechanics of inflation targeting rely on the central bank's ability to influence aggregate demand through adjustments to the cash rate, the overnight interbank lending rate. When inflation threatens to exceed the target band, the RBA raises the cash rate, increasing borrowing costs across the economy and dampening spending. Conversely, when economic activity weakens and inflation falls below target, rate cuts stimulate borrowing and investment. The transmission mechanism, however, is neither instantaneous nor uniform — monetary policy operates with long and variable lags.

The post-pandemic period has tested the inflation-targeting framework in unprecedented ways. Supply chain disruptions, energy price shocks, and the unwinding of extraordinary fiscal and monetary stimulus combined to push inflation well above the target band in 2022 and 2023. The RBA's response — a rapid series of rate increases from historic lows — generated significant debate about the appropriate pace and magnitude of tightening, particularly given the impact on mortgage holders and renters.

Looking ahead, the inflation-targeting framework faces several structural challenges. The neutral rate of interest may have shifted, climate-related supply shocks are likely to become more frequent, and the interaction between monetary and fiscal policy has become more complex. Whether the current framework proves sufficiently flexible to navigate these challenges, or whether a more fundamental rethinking of central bank mandates is required, remains an open and important question for Australian macroeconomic policy.
