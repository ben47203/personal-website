Geopolitics of Water Scarcity█

November 2025 Geography

Water scarcity is increasingly recognised as a driver of geopolitical tension, population displacement, and interstate conflict. With approximately four billion people experiencing severe water scarcity for at least one month per year, and climate change intensifying hydrological variability, competition over freshwater resources has emerged as a defining challenge of the 21st century. This essay explores how water scarcity shapes international relations in three critical regions.

The Nile Basin illustrates how upstream infrastructure development can generate downstream anxiety. Ethiopia's Grand Renaissance Dam, under construction since 2011, will be Africa's largest hydroelectric facility — but Egypt, which depends on the Nile for 97 per cent of its freshwater, views the dam as an existential threat. Negotiations have stalled repeatedly over filling timelines and drought protocols, with Egypt's foreign minister describing the situation as "a matter of life and death." The case demonstrates how shared river systems create structural interdependence that existing international law inadequately governs.

In Central Asia, the collapse of Soviet water-sharing agreements following independence left five states competing over the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers. Upstream Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan seek hydroelectric development; downstream Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and Kazakhstan require irrigation flows for cotton agriculture. The resulting tensions — including border closures, trade restrictions, and military posturing — illustrate how water scarcity interacts with post-colonial state formation to generate instability.

The Middle East presents perhaps the starkest case, where water scarcity compounds existing territorial conflicts. Israeli control of West Bank aquifers provides Palestinians with approximately one-quarter of the per capita water consumption available to Israeli settlers — a disparity that both reflects and reinforces the broader occupation. Water infrastructure has become simultaneously a tool of territorial control and a site of resistance, demonstrating that resource scarcity is never purely technical but always embedded in political structures.
