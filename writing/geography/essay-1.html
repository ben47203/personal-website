Urbanisation and Sustainability in SEQ█

August 2025 Geography

South East Queensland is one of Australia's fastest-growing regions, with population projections suggesting an additional 1.9 million residents by 2046. This rapid urbanisation presents fundamental challenges for sustainable development: how can the region accommodate growth while preserving ecological systems, maintaining liveability, and building resilience to climate impacts? This essay analyses the competing pressures shaping SEQ's urban future.

The region's growth has historically followed a car-dependent, low-density suburban model, consuming agricultural land and bushland at rates that outpace population growth. Between 2001 and 2021, SEQ's urban footprint expanded by approximately 40 per cent while population grew by 35 per cent — indicating sprawl rather than densification. This pattern generates cascading sustainability challenges: increased transport emissions, habitat fragmentation, higher infrastructure costs per capita, and greater exposure to bushfire and flood risk at the urban-rural interface.

The ShapingSEQ regional plan attempts to redirect growth toward consolidation within existing urban areas, transit-oriented development, and protection of inter-urban breaks. However, implementation faces persistent obstacles: community resistance to densification, the economics of greenfield development favouring developers, and coordination failures across the eleven local government areas that comprise the region.

Climate adaptation adds urgency to these planning challenges. SEQ faces compounding risks from sea level rise affecting coastal suburbs, intensifying rainfall events overwhelming drainage infrastructure designed for historical conditions, and urban heat island effects that make densification itself a health risk without adequate green infrastructure. The region's sustainability depends on integrating these adaptation requirements into growth management rather than treating them as separate concerns.
