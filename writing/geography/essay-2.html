Climate Change Impacts on the Great Barrier Reef█

October 2025 Geography

The Great Barrier Reef, the world's largest coral reef system spanning over 2,300 kilometres along Australia's northeast coast, faces an existential threat from anthropogenic climate change. Rising ocean temperatures, acidification, and intensifying cyclones have driven unprecedented mass bleaching events, fundamentally altering the reef's ecological structure. This essay documents these impacts and evaluates the prospects for reef resilience under current emission trajectories.

Coral bleaching occurs when thermal stress causes corals to expel the symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) that provide them with energy and colour. The reef experienced mass bleaching events in 1998, 2002, 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2022 — with the frequency accelerating as baseline ocean temperatures rise. The 2016 event alone killed approximately 30 per cent of the reef's shallow-water corals, with the northern third most severely affected. Recovery between events requires a minimum of 10-15 years, a timeframe increasingly unlikely as bleaching intervals compress.

Ocean acidification presents a slower but potentially more fundamental threat. As atmospheric CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it reduces the availability of carbonate ions essential for coral skeleton formation. Under high-emission scenarios, reef waters may become net dissolving for coral structures by mid-century, undermining the physical framework that supports the entire ecosystem. Unlike bleaching, acidification damage is not reversible on human timescales.

Current interventions — crown-of-thorns starfish control, water quality improvement, localised coral gardening — address proximate stressors but cannot compensate for ongoing warming. The scientific consensus is clear: the reef's long-term survival depends primarily on global emission reductions sufficient to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. Without this, management efforts amount to palliative care for an ecosystem in terminal decline.
